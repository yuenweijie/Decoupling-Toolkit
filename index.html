Not sure who should be the staying vs exiting party? Tap for guidance →

Decoupling frees up one person's name so they can buy the next property as a "first" purchase. Use these to decide the roles:

Exiting party — sells their share & buys next
  • The one who plans to buy the next property — decoupling clears their name for a fresh purchase.
  • Ideally a Singapore Citizen — their next home is then treated as a first property (0% ABSD). A PR pays 5%, a foreigner 60% — so if only one party is a Citizen, that person usually exits.
  • Strong income & preferably younger age — needed to qualify for the new loan (TDSR; and age + tenure ≤ 65 for maximum leverage).
  • Frees up useful cash + CPF for the next down payment.
Staying party — keeps the property & takes over the full loan
  • Must qualify for and afford the FULL outstanding loan on their own — their income has to pass TDSR on the restructured loan. This is the hard gate.
  • Wants to keep this property (to live in or hold).
  • Has enough cash + CPF to fund the buyout (the exiting party's share + stamp duty).
Pointers to note
  1. Can the other party hold the loan alone? If not, swap the roles — or decoupling won't work.
  2. If only one is a Citizen, the Citizen usually exits to avoid ABSD on the next purchase.
  3. Mind the CPF split — the exiting party must refund all their CPF used + accrued interest in full, no matter how small their share. So don't put the party who used a lot of CPF on a tiny 1% share — their small proceeds won't cover the refund, forcing a big cash top-up.

Watch-out: if the intended staying party can't service the full loan on their own income, the decoupling fails — reconsider the roles, or whether to decouple at all.
